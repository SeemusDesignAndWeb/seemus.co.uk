Issue 28

New legislation

1. Which of the following statements, based on the digest of the Criminal Procedure Rules 2025 (SI 2025/909) (item 39), is incorrect?
  • Part 2 (understanding and applying the rules; powers of authorised court officers and justices' legal advisers) is amended to allow an authorised court officer in the Crown Court to amend a community order or a suspended sentence order where a probation officer has allowed an offender to change the local justice area in which they reside.
  • Part 3 (case management) is amended to require routine consideration of reporting and comparable restrictions during preparation for trial.
  • Part 6 (reporting, etc restrictions) is amended to ensure that a prospective court reporter is recognised as a person directly affected by a reporting restriction, and to accommodate arrangements that facilitate notice to such a reporter of applications relating to reporting restrictions.
  • Part 42 (appeal to the Court of Appeal in confiscation and related proceedings) is amended to require service of a notice of appeal in confiscation proceedings on the Crown Court officer.

Substantive law/Miscellaneous cases

4. Which of the following statements is incorrect?
  • In Cetin v Epping Forest DC (item 5) the Upper Tribunal held that, in section 263(3) of the Housing Act 2004, the definition of "person managing" is intended to identify someone who undertakes management responsibilities and to make them subject to relevant statutory obligations, backed by criminal and civil sanctions, and it cannot have been intended that the receipt of a single instalment of rent by an agent who has no continuing management responsibilities should be enough to place the agent under the same statutory obligations as a landlord or a managing agent.
  • In Portugal v dos Santos (item 6) the court held that nothing in section 20 of the Extradition Act 2003, which inter alia concerns the right of a requested person convicted in absentia to a retrial, indicates that the right to call evidence at a retrial should be subject to any substantive qualification beyond the requirements that would apply before evidence could be called at the original trial.
  • In R. (Tompson) v Secretary of State for Justice (item 8), where the Secretary of State rejected the Parole Board's recommendation to transfer the claimant (a post-tariff sex offender serving a discretionary life sentence) to open conditions in light of new information including allegations made against him by another prisoner, it had not been procedurally unfair for the Secretary of State not to convene an oral hearing to allow the claimant to answer the allegations and to permit their closer examination.
  • The court went on to comment that, in most cases where new information comes to light after a Parole Board hearing, the Secretary of State, with the expertise of her department, is likely to be able to address its significance in asking the question of how it bears on the risk posed by the prisoner if he moves to the open estate.

5. Which of the following statements is incorrect?
  • In R. v Odunuga (item 1), where the appellant was convicted of murder having caused three serious wounds to the victim with a particularly dangerous knife, the judge had been entitled to rule that manslaughter was not an alternative verdict that was realistically available to the jury on the evidence.
  • In R. v BSH (item 3) the court ruled that, on the correct construction of rule 50(1) of the Armed Forces (Court Martial) Rules 2009 (SI 2009/2041) (CLW/09/29/39), the prosecution did not have a right of appeal against the ruling made by the Judge Advocate General dismissing the case against the defendant.
  • In R. v Mingo (item 5) the court held that the judge's direction to the jury in response to a question from them, "What is the definition of reasonable doubt? Anything other than a 100% sure?", that "Members of the jury, you are wrong to equate my direction of proof beyond reasonable doubt with 100% sure. If you read my directions again, they are clearly set out. My direction is that you must be sure. I do not say 100% sure. 100% sure would, you may think, equate with certainty and certainty is not the standard of proof required. …", could not have caused the jury to think that they could convict a defendant even though they were not sure of his guilt.
  • The court went on to comment that it would have been better if the judge had not introduced the word "certainty" into her direction when the jury had not used that word.

6. Which of the following statements, based on the digest of R. v Hurley (item 4) and setting out the current position on the admissibility of false complaint evidence in sexual offence cases, is incorrect?
  • Evidence that a complainant has made false complaints of rape on occasions other than those on the indictment is always non-defendant bad character evidence for the purposes of section 100 of the Criminal Justice Act 2003, because it is evidence of misconduct as defined in section 112(1) of the 2003 Act as "the commission of an offence or other reprehensible behaviour".
  • Therefore, admissibility requires meeting the enhanced relevance test under section 100(1)(b) of the 2003 Act, which mandates that (1) the evidence must have substantial (but not necessarily conclusive) probative value in relation to the complainant's credibility, and (2) such credibility must be a matter in issue of substantial importance in the proceedings as a whole.
  • False complaints where the complainant claims to be the victim of other sexual offending will engage section 41 of the Youth Justice and Criminal Evidence Act 1999 if the evidence is "about" the complainant's "sexual behaviour" for section 41 purposes, but where the questioning is not about any sexual activity of the complainant, but about what the complainant said, then section 41 will not be engaged and the admissibility issue will be resolved applying section 100 of the 2003 Act.
  • Before section 41 can be avoided on this basis, there must be "a proper evidential basis" for concluding that the complaint was false, which can be no less than a strong factual foundation indicative of falsity.

  • The court added that section 43(1) does not have the effect of otherwise constraining the powers of the Court of Appeal, and the omission of any reference to the crediting of time spent on bail subject to a qualifying curfew condition does not affect that position.

Issue 31

Evidence and procedure

9. Which of the following statements, based on the digest of R. (Malone) v Llandudno Magistrates' Court (item 1), is incorrect?
  • While the reasons for a decision to adjourn or to decline an adjournment request can be succinct, they must be sufficiently detailed to enable the parties to understand the reasons for the decision and for a reviewing court to see that the magistrates exercised their discretion on the correct legal basis.
  • Where the High Court finds that a decision to grant an adjournment was unlawful, it has a wide discretion in deciding whether to (a) quash the decision and remit the matter with a direction to acquit, or (b) quash the decision and remit the matter for the case to continue.
  • In circumstances where the prosecution did not concede at the time that it would offer no evidence if the adjournment application were to be refused and where it did not accept on appeal that the continuation of the trial at that time would necessarily have resulted in an acquittal, considerable caution would be needed before the court could remit with a direction to acquit.
  • The fact that the need for an adjournment was brought about by prosecution failings was not in itself relevant to the question of the basis on which the matter should be remitted.

11. Which of the following statements, based on the digest of R. v Norman (item 4) concerning the application of section 74(3) of the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984, which provides that where a defendant is proved to have been convicted of a previous offence in the UK he shall be taken to have committed that offence unless the contrary is proved, is incorrect?
  • There are no hard and fast rules about what evidence may or may not be adduced by a defendant, but the prosecution should be in a position to decide how to respond to the case that the defendant can advance, which may involve the prosecution calling its evidence in advance of the defendant giving theirs or the prosecution being permitted to postpone their decision about how to react.
  • Depending upon the facts of the case, it may be appropriate for the trial judge to offer comment on the limitations (and, by parity of reasoning, the strengths) of the evidence that the defendant has put forward, but once properly raised the issue will be for the jury to decide.
  • Substantial evidence is required to prove that the defendant did not commit the earlier offence, and the fact that the evidence comes from the defendant alone or that such evidence amounts to no more than his bare statement precludes reliance on section 74(3).
  • Where, in that case, the appellant was being retried for five counts of indecency with a child, the judge had erred in directing the jury that previous convictions in relation to lesser offences at the original trial involving the same complainant were a given: that could only have placed the appellant at a severe and unfair disadvantage when the jury came to consider the retrial counts, and the retrial jury had to assess the credibility of the complainant and the appellant for themselves.

12. Which of the following statements is incorrect?
  • In R. v Tallentire and anor (item 1) it was held that a judge may, as a matter of discretion, and depending on their assessment of fairness in the particular circumstances, comment on the failure of the defence to call a witness, but if a judge is intending to make such a comment, counsel for the defence should be given an opportunity in the absence of the jury to address whether the judge should in fact make a comment, and if the judge does make a comment the judge should remind the jury that there is no burden of proof on the defendant.
  • The court added that, while what fairness requires will obviously depend on the circumstances, it will not be fair to make an adverse comment about the failure to call a witness who is not available to be called.
  • In R. v Evans (item 2) a person employed as an "operational support grade" (OSG) at a prison (a position that was not a prison officer) was held not to be a public officer for the purposes of the offence of misconduct in public office.
  • The comment to that case highlights the Law Commission's 2020 recommendation to replace the current common law offence of misconduct in public office with two better defined statutory offences: corruption in public office and breach of duty in public office.

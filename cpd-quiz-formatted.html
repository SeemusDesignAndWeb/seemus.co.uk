6. Which of the following statements, based on the digest of Andrysiewicz v Circuit Court in Lodz, Poland (item 2), is incorrect?
  • Cases in which a submission founded on Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights may defeat the public interest in extradition will be rare.
  • The incidence in reliance on the Article 8 "defence" will only decrease through robust case management directions and an appreciation by the legal aid authorities as to the hurdle that must be surpassed before deciding to make public funds available to advance such a defence.
  • However, where early release from a sentence under the law of the requesting state will operate automatically so that early release can be precisely calculated and predicted, Article 8 may well outweigh the public interest in extradition, notwithstanding the fact that the requested person may be subject to licence conditions if extradited.
  • Although it is unrealistic not to recognise the existence of the discretionary early release provisions in the Polish Penal Code, ordinarily it will only be appropriate to take account of the bare possibility of early release in Poland, which will add little weight in determining whether extradition is a disproportionate interference with Article 8.

7. Which of the following statements is incorrect?

  • The court commented that the ECJ's decision in Proceedings concerning LU, which is authority for the proposition that, if a court activates a suspended sentence because of a subsequent conviction, then that conviction must itself arise from an Article 6 compliant procedure, is a decision sitting uneasily between the established model of extradition based on conviction and sentence, and one demanding investigation of the fairness of subsequent discretionary decision-making about the revocation of conditional liberty.

  • The Criminal Justice Act 1988 (Offensive Weapons) (Amendment, Surrender and Compensation) (England and Wales) Order 2025 (SI 2025/681) (item 5) amends the schedule to the Criminal Justice Act 1988 (Offensive Weapons) Order 1988 (SI 1988/2019), which specifies weapons and defences for the purposes of the summary offences under section 141 of the 1988 Act, to add the weapon sometimes known as a "ninja sword".

Issue 23

Evidence and procedure

8. Which of the following statements is incorrect?
  • In R. v Carroll (item 2) the trial judge was held to have fallen into error when he sought to limit the manner in which two witnesses called on behalf of the co-accused could be cross-examined on behalf of the appellant by preventing counsel from asking them leading rather than open questions.
  • The comment to that case points out, by way of analogy with the examination of vulnerable witnesses, that counsel can properly examine a witness using open questions, even though it goes against the mantra that one must always "lead".
  • In R. (DPP) v Crown Court at Stoke-on-Trent; Reaney (item 4) the court held that adjournments occasioned by the lack of availability of court rooms and judges, including for resource reasons, cannot amount to a good and sufficient cause for the custody time limit to be extended.
  • Where, in that case, there had been delays in serving the case papers and disclosure on the self-represented interested party in prison, the court commented that it may have been preferable to have listed the matter and handed the papers to the interested party when he was produced at court.

9. Which of the following statements is incorrect?
  • In R. v BAP and ors (item 6) it was held that a submission of "no case to answer" can legitimately be made in an appropriate "reverse burden" case, but is likely to be exceptionally rare.
  • In that case, concerning the offence of supply of water not fit for human consumption contrary to section 70 of the Water Industry Act 1991 which is subject to a due diligence defence, the judge had been entitled to accede to submissions of no case to answer at the close of the prosecution case on the basis that the prosecution had failed to disprove that the defendants had exercised due diligence.
  • In Jobson v DPP (item 7) the court held that, although Candlish v DPP was a case concerning aggregation, it is also authority for the more general proposition that low-value shoplifting is an indictable offence unless and until the magistrates' court decides at the first appearance (when the value and aggregate value of the item or items stolen are determined) that it is not, as a result of which the time limitation provisions for summary offences in section 127 of the Magistrates' Courts Act 1980 do not apply.
  • The court went on to state that, while section 22A(1) of the 1980 Act states that an offence of low-value shoplifting is triable only summarily, that subsection cannot be read in isolation, and section 22A(2), under which a defendant can elect to be tried in the Crown Court, immediately makes clear that the proposition in section 22A(1) is not an absolute position.
